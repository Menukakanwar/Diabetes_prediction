About the Parameters



  • Age: The age of the patient. Age is a risk factor because the likelihood of developing diabetes increases as you get older.
  • Gender Men - Men are slightly more likely to develop type 2 diabetes at an earlier age compared to women, though this may vary based on lifestyle factors.

    Women - Postmenopausal women may face a higher risk due to hormonal changes, and women with a history of gestational diabetes have an elevated risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.
  • Glucose: Plasma glucose concentration after a 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test. High glucose levels are a primary indicator of diabetes.
  • Blood Pressure: Diastolic blood pressure (mm Hg). High blood pressure is associated with an increased risk of diabetes and its complications.
  • Heart Disease Individuals with heart disease often have risk factors that overlap with those for diabetes, such as high blood pressure, obesity, and abnormal cholesterol levels. The presence of heart disease indicates higher risks of metabolic syndrome, which is strongly linked to diabetes.
  • BMI: Body Mass Index (weight in kg/(height in m)^2). Higher BMI values indicate obesity, which is a major risk factor for diabetes.
  • Smoking History Smoking is a major risk factor for diabetes. Smokers are more likely to develop insulin resistance, which can lead to type 2 diabetes. Smoking also worsens complications in people who already have diabetes, such as cardiovascular issues.
  • Hypertension Hypertension is a strong predictor of diabetes because high blood pressure is often associated with insulin resistance. Both conditions share similar risk factors, like obesity and inactivity, which increase the likelihood of developing diabetes.
  • HbA1c HbA1c is a long-term marker of blood glucose levels, providing an average of blood sugar control over the past 2-3 months.
